# Decision Brief

This folder contains the decision brief template — the exec-ready output
that goes to CIO, GM of Technology and Operations, Finance, and Procurement
at the conclusion of the five workshops.

---

## Who this document is for

The decision brief is written for people who were not in the workshops.
They need to understand the methodology, trust the numbers, and make a
decision — without having to read five workshops worth of working documents.

That means every section must:

- State its conclusions clearly before explaining how it got there
- Reference the source without requiring the reader to go there
- Carry a confidence rating wherever a number is based on an estimate
- Connect cost findings to value findings so the picture is always balanced

---

## When to produce it

The decision brief is produced after Workshop 5 and reviewed with the
platform team before it goes to the leadership audience.

Do not send it to leadership before the platform team has reviewed it.
The brief represents what was agreed in the workshops — the platform team
should recognise everything in it. If something is wrong or misrepresented,
it is better to catch it in review than in the boardroom.

---

## How to produce it

The decision brief is populated by carrying forward outputs from the
working documents in the correct order:

```
Assumptions Register          → Methodology section + Appendix
Data Request List             → Appendix
Cost Taxonomy Baseline        → Cost Baseline section
Unit Metrics Definition       → Unit Economics section
Showback View                 → Cost Baseline section (supporting)
Value and Risk Scorecard      → Value and Risk section
Workload Placement Matrix     → Scenario Comparison section (supporting)
Scenario Comparison           → Scenario Comparison + Recommendation sections
90-Day Plan                   → 90-Day Plan section
```

Every section of the decision brief template carries a pointer to the
working document it draws from. Follow those pointers. Do not summarise
from memory — carry the agreed numbers forward.

---

## Tone and language

The decision brief is written for a mixed audience — technical leadership,
finance, and procurement. The language should be:

- Plain and direct — no jargon that Finance will not recognise
- Confident but honest — confidence ratings are a feature, not a weakness
- Balanced — cost and value findings sit alongside each other throughout
- Defensible — every number has a source, every assumption has a reason

---

## Length

A decision brief that requires more than twenty pages to make its case
is too long. Aim for:

- Executive summary: 1 to 2 pages
- Methodology: 1 page
- Cost baseline: 2 to 3 pages
- Unit economics: 1 to 2 pages
- Value scorecard: 1 to 2 pages
- Scenario comparison: 2 to 3 pages
- Recommendation and 90-day plan: 2 pages
- Appendix: as needed — this is the reference section, not the argument

Total: 10 to 15 pages, excluding appendix.
